# Signal-Preserving 2/3-Approximation for Explainable Linear Information Design


## Background


In one-dimensional **linear information design**, a state $\theta \in [0,1]$ is drawn from a prior distribution $F$, and a signaling scheme induces a distribution over posterior means $\mu = \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid s]$. The designer's payoff from signal $s$ (with posterior mean $\mu$) is $u(\mu)$, where $u:[0,1]\to[0,1]$ is an arbitrary interim utility function.


A general $K$-signal scheme may randomize or pool disconnected subsets of states into the same signal. Such schemes can be optimal but hard to explain or audit. A natural explainable alternative is a **$K$-interval-partitional signaling scheme**: choose thresholds
$$
0=a_0\le a_1\le \cdots \le a_K=1,
$$
and send one signal for each interval $[a_{i-1},a_i]$. Under the uniform prior, the posterior mean induced by interval $[a_{i-1},a_i]$ is $(a_{i-1}+a_i)/2$.


Let $\mathrm{OPT}_I(K)$ be the optimal designer payoff over all signaling schemes with at most $K$ signals, and let $\mathrm{OPT}^{\mathrm{part}}_I(K)$ be the optimal payoff over $K$-interval-partitional signaling schemes.


Chen, Lin, Tang, and Tucker-Foltz (EC 2026) show that for the uniform prior, interval-partitional schemes achieve a tight $2/3$-approximation to arbitrary $K$-signal schemes if the partitional scheme is allowed to use more signals: specifically, if $K'\ge 3K/2$, then
$$
\mathrm{OPT}^{\mathrm{part}}_I(K')\ge \frac{2}{3} \mathrm{OPT}_I(K).
$$
Their proof starts from an optimal $K$-signal scheme, uses a bi-pooling characterization of optimal schemes, and converts each two-signal bi-pooling interval into a three-interval partitional scheme. Importantly, this conversion process increases the total number of signals from $K$ to at most $3K/2$.


## Open Question


Is the signal increase necessary to achieve the $2/3$ approximation?


Formally, is it true that for every integer $K\ge 2$, every utility function $u:[0,1]\to[0,1]$, and prior $F=\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$,
$$
\mathrm{OPT}^{\mathrm{part}}_I(K)\ge \frac{2}{3} \mathrm{OPT}_I(K)?
$$
One approach is to see if every arbitrary $K$-signal scheme under the uniform prior can be converted into a $K$-interval-partitional scheme while losing at most a factor $1/3$ of the payoff.


If yes, then the $2/3$-approximation result combined with the negative result in [Chen, Lin, Tang, Tucker-Foltz, EC 2026] will imply a tight price of explainability bound of $2/3$ for any fixed number of signals.


## Why This Problem May Be Suitable for AI-Assisted Research


This problem is a concrete technical logjam. The known proof achieves the tight $2/3$ ratio but only by increasing the number of signals. The remaining difficulty is local and geometric: inside a bi-pooling interval, two posterior means generated by an arbitrary scheme can be approximated by three consecutive intervals, but it is unknown whether two intervals suffice, or whether losses can be compensated across different bi-pooling intervals.


Possible AI-assisted approaches include:


1. searching symbolically for sharper two-interval conversion inequalities;
2. using computational experiments to find extremal utility functions and counterexamples for small $K$;
3. formulating the problem as a finite-dimensional nonlinear or linear program after discretization;
4. discovering new global conversion arguments that reallocate intervals across different bi-pooling components.
